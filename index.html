NanoGPT Speedrun: Activation Rankings Depend on the Optimizer

Adam Holmes · June 2026 · modded-nanogpt fork

Key findings — two statistically significant results from 124M-parameter runs under Muon + Polar Express:
  1. Gated MLPs reverse from best to worst when switching AdamW → Muon. Under AdamW, gating beats standard MLPs by ~0.09 nats. Under Muon, all four gated variants are significantly worse (p<0.05, n=4, Δ = +0.10 to +0.16 nats).
  2. SNIQU significantly beats ReLU² under Muon (p<0.0001, n=9, Δ = −0.076 nats, all 9 seeds same direction). SNIQU is a new activation we designed for this study — details below.
A single-seed NorMuon screen suggests both effects may not persist under the production optimizer — all three activations landed within 0.012 nats at the same optimal LR. A powered multi-seed NorMuon study is underway; see Section 5.

1. Background

Modded-NanoGPT is a community speedrun: train a ~124M-parameter GPT on FineWeb to val loss ≤3.28 nats as fast as possible on 8×H100. The record is ~1.4 minutes, using Muon, FP8 matmuls, sliding-window attention, and ReLU² (max(0,x)²) in the MLP blocks.

We asked whether changing the activation could improve on the record — and found a more interesting answer: activation and gating rankings change dramatically depending on which optimizer you use.

2. Experimental setup

Model: decoder-only GPT at two scales — medium (51M: 8 layers, width 512) for fast pilots, small (124M: 12 layers, width 768, 6 heads) matching real nanogpt-small for the main study.
Optimizers: AdamW, Muon (orthogonalizes each weight-matrix update via the Polar Express iteration), and NorMuon (Muon + per-neuron variance normalization).
Training: bf16, seq 1024, batch 16, 4000 steps, 4–9 seeds per variant. Variants share seeds so comparisons are paired — per-seed noise ~0.004 vs run-to-run variance ~0.05. Significance via paired t-tests.
Same as benchmark: model size, Muon + Polar Express, torch.compile, chunked cross-entropy. Not included: FP8, value embeddings, sliding-window attention. Good for ranking; validating on the real benchmark needs rented H100s.

3. What we compared

The MLP block is either standard (W₂·act(W₁x)) or gated (W₃·[act(W₁x) ⊙ (W₂x)] — two projections multiplied, the basis of SwiGLU/GeGLU in modern LLMs). Activations tested: ReLU², GELU, SiLU, x|x| (signed square), bilinear (no activation), and variants with a learnable slope or curvature.

The pattern forms a clean 2×2: shape change alone (iqu) fails; moment-matching alone (selu) fails; only their combination (sniqu) wins. Self-normalization is the active ingredient.

Designing sniqu

We started with iqu — a new C² piecewise activation: x + x² for x ≥ 0, and x/(1−x) for x < 0. No dead zone, costs one reciprocal (no transcendentals), and value/slope/curvature all match at the origin. We thought the interesting nonlinear shape might help under Muon.

It didn't — iqu is a confirmed null (p=0.88, n=9). But the reason pointed to a fix. For x ∼ N(0,1), iqu's output has mean ≈ 0.71 and variance ≈ 3.19: every MLP layer multiplies the residual stream scale by √3.19 ≈ 1.8× and adds a DC offset. Under AdamW this doesn't matter much — AdamW adapts per-parameter. Under Muon it does: Muon orthogonalizes each weight-matrix update, and a non-zero activation mean injects a rank-1 "DC" direction into the gradient that Muon treats as genuine signal, degrading conditioning.

The fix is the same trick SELU used: solve for the shift β and scale λ that make the output zero-mean, unit-variance under Gaussian input. Compute iqu's moments by Gauss–Hermite quadrature — μ = E[h] = 0.706, σ² = Var[h] = 3.188 — then define:

sniqu(x) = λ (h(x) − β),    β = 0.706,   λ = 0.560.

By construction, E[sniqu] = 0 and Var[sniqu] = 1. And it works: sniqu beats relu² by 0.076 nats under Muon (p<0.0001, all 9 seeds), while iqu (same shape, unnormalized) is a null. The normalization — not the shape — is the active ingredient.

6. Why the optimizer reorders activations

AdamW rescales each weight's update by its own gradient magnitude, making it forgiving of activation scale and conditioning. Under AdamW, architectures compete on raw expressivity — gating adds multiplicative interactions and wins.

Muon replaces each update with its nearest orthogonal matrix. This is powerful but sensitive to gradient conditioning, which the activation controls. Two consequences: (1) plain standard MLPs train much better under Muon (loss drops from 6.93 to 6.20), closing the gap gating was filling; (2) activation scale and mean now matter directly, since Muon can't freely rescale them the way AdamW can.

The whole activation canon — GELU (2016), SwiGLU (2020) — was established under Adam. These results show those rankings don't automatically carry over to Muon.

7. Final conclusions

  • Gating is out. Every gated MLP we tried is significantly worse than plain ReLU² under Muon at 124M — and costs more compute on top of that (p<0.05, n=4, all four variants). Not worth touching.
  • sniqu is the one activation that actually helps under Muon (p<0.0001, Δ=−0.076, all 9 seeds pointing the same way). The shape change alone (iqu) does nothing — it's the self-normalization that matters. Whether the win survives NorMuon is still open: a quick single-seed screen showed all three activations within 0.012 nats of each other, which is suspicious, but we need a proper multi-seed run before concluding anything.
  • The infrastructure is there if we want to follow up: sniqu and relu2_s1 are already wired into the real train_gpt.py behind a one-line env flag, the fused Triton kernel works, and there's a turnkey H100 A/B harness in deploy/. If the NorMuon study shows a signal, it would be easy to test on the actual benchmark.
  • The bigger takeaway: activation rankings are optimizer-dependent in a pretty striking way. Gating beats standard MLPs by ~0.09 nats under AdamW, then loses by 0.10–0.16 under Muon. The whole activation canon — GELU, SwiGLU, all of it — was established with Adam. These results suggest you can't just assume those rankings carry over to Muon or other orthogonalizing optimizers, which is worth knowing given how much the optimizer landscape has shifted recently.
