NanoGPT Speedrun: Activation Rankings Depend on the Optimizer

Adam Holmes · June 2026 · modded-nanogpt fork

Key findings — a clean 2×2 from matched 124M-parameter runs (same scale and steps, optimizer is the only variable):
  1. Gating reverses: wins under AdamW, loses under Muon. Gated MLPs beat relu2 by 0.06–0.11 nats under AdamW (p≤0.001) and lose by 0.10–0.16 nats under Muon (p<0.05, n=4). Same activations, same scale, opposite sign.
  2. SNIQU reverses too: wins under Muon, loses under AdamW. SNIQU beats relu2 by 0.076 nats under Muon (p<0.0001, n=9, all seeds same direction) and loses by ~0.18–0.29 nats under AdamW. SNIQU is a new C² activation I designed for this study — details below.
A single-seed NorMuon screen suggests both effects may not persist under the production optimizer — all three activations landed within 0.012 nats at the same optimal LR. A powered multi-seed NorMuon study is underway; see Section 5.

1. Background

Modded-NanoGPT is a community speedrun: train a ~124M-parameter GPT on FineWeb to val loss ≤3.28 nats as fast as possible on 8×H100. The record is ~1.4 minutes, using Muon, FP8 matmuls, sliding-window attention, and ReLU² (max(0,x)²) in the MLP blocks.

I asked whether changing the activation could improve on the record — and found a more interesting answer: activation and gating rankings change dramatically depending on which optimizer you use.

2. Experimental setup

Model: decoder-only GPT at two scales — medium (51M: 8 layers, width 512) for fast pilots, small (124M: 12 layers, width 768, 6 heads) matching real nanogpt-small for the main study.
Optimizers: AdamW, Muon (orthogonalizes each weight-matrix update via the Polar Express iteration), and NorMuon (Muon + per-neuron variance normalization).
Training: bf16, seq 1024, batch 16, 4000 steps, 4–9 seeds per variant. Variants share seeds so comparisons are paired — per-seed noise ~0.004 vs run-to-run variance ~0.05. Significance via paired t-tests.
Same as benchmark: model size, Muon + Polar Express, torch.compile, chunked cross-entropy. Not included: FP8, value embeddings, sliding-window attention. Good for ranking; validating on the real benchmark needs rented H100s.

3. What I compared

The MLP block is either standard (W₂·act(W₁x)) or gated (W₃·[act(W₁x) ⊙ (W₂x)] — two projections multiplied, the basis of SwiGLU/GeGLU in modern LLMs). Activations tested: ReLU², GELU, SiLU, x|x| (signed square), bilinear (no activation), and variants with a learnable slope or curvature.

Designing sniqu

I started with iqu — a new C² piecewise activation: x + x² for x ≥ 0, and x/(1−x) for x < 0. No dead zone, costs one reciprocal (no transcendentals), and value/slope/curvature all match at the origin. I thought the interesting nonlinear shape might help under Muon.

It didn't — iqu is a confirmed null (p=0.88, n=9). But the reason pointed to a fix. For x ∼ N(0,1), iqu's output has mean ≈ 0.71 and variance ≈ 3.19: every MLP layer multiplies the residual stream scale by √3.19 ≈ 1.8× and adds a DC offset. Under AdamW this doesn't matter much — AdamW adapts per-parameter. Under Muon it does: Muon orthogonalizes each weight-matrix update, and a non-zero activation mean injects a rank-1 "DC" direction into the gradient that Muon treats as genuine signal, degrading conditioning.

The fix is the same trick SELU used: solve for the shift β and scale λ that make the output zero-mean, unit-variance under Gaussian input. Compute iqu's moments by Gauss–Hermite quadrature — μ = E[h] = 0.706, σ² = Var[h] = 3.188 — then define:

sniqu(x) = λ (h(x) − β),    β = 0.706,   λ = 0.560.

By construction, E[sniqu] = 0 and Var[sniqu] = 1. And it works: sniqu beats relu² by 0.076 nats under Muon (p<0.0001, all 9 seeds), while iqu (same shape, unnormalized) is a null. The normalization — not the shape — is the active ingredient.

6. Why the optimizer reorders activations

AdamW normalizes each parameter's update by its own gradient RMS, making it forgiving of scale and conditioning. Under AdamW, architectures compete on raw expressivity: gating adds multiplicative interactions and wins; ReLU²'s ~50% dead zone concentrates gradient signal onto fewer neurons, keeping updates informative.

Muon replaces each weight matrix's update with its nearest orthogonal matrix. This is powerful but makes two things matter that AdamW ignores:

Why gating loses under Muon. Gating's multiplicative interaction entangles W₁ and W₂'s gradients — the useful update direction for each depends on what the other is currently doing. Muon orthogonalizes each matrix independently, spreading energy into directions that weren't meaningfully updated by either. The joint credit assignment that gating requires is exactly what per-matrix orthogonalization can't handle. On top of that, plain standard MLPs already train much better under Muon than AdamW (loss drops from 6.93 to 6.20 in these runs), closing the expressivity gap that gating was filling.

Why sniqu wins over relu2 under Muon but not AdamW. A non-zero activation mean injects a rank-1 "DC" direction into the gradient that Muon treats as genuine signal, degrading conditioning. ReLU²'s output mean (~0.57 for standard-normal input) creates exactly this contamination; sniqu eliminates it by design. Under AdamW the per-parameter normalization handles mean and scale automatically, so sniqu's moment-matching buys nothing — and relu2's dead zone becomes an asset, concentrating gradient signal and acting as a natural stabilizer at higher LRs.

The one-sentence summary: AdamW rewards expressivity and sparsity; Muon rewards conditioning and scale control. Every result in this study follows from that.

An open conjecture. Gating's problem under Muon is the entangled gradients between the gate and value branches. A natural fix would be to use AdamW on the gate weights and Muon on the value/projection weights. The gate is a routing mechanism with skewed gradient statistics — exactly where AdamW's per-parameter adaptation helps most. This is the same logic behind the record stack already using AdamW on embeddings and Muon on weight matrices. Whether such a hybrid could recover gating's AdamW advantage under Muon is an open question worth testing cheaply.

The whole activation canon — GELU (2016), SwiGLU (2020) — was established under Adam. These results show those rankings don't automatically carry over to Muon.

7. Final conclusions

  • Gating is out. Every gated MLP I tried is significantly worse than plain ReLU² under Muon at 124M — and costs more compute on top of that (p<0.05, n=4, all four variants). Not worth touching.
  • sniqu is the one activation that actually helps under Muon (p<0.0001, Δ=−0.076, all 9 seeds pointing the same way). The shape change alone (iqu) does nothing — it's the self-normalization that matters. Whether the win survives NorMuon is still open: a quick single-seed screen showed all three activations within 0.012 nats of each other, which is suspicious, but a proper multi-seed run is needed before concluding anything.
  • The infrastructure is there to follow up: sniqu and relu2_s1 are already wired into the real train_gpt.py behind a one-line env flag, the fused Triton kernel works, and there's a turnkey H100 A/B harness in deploy/. If the NorMuon study shows a signal, it would be easy to test on the actual benchmark.
  • The bigger takeaway: it's a clean 2×2. Gating wins under AdamW, loses under Muon. SNIQU wins under Muon, loses under AdamW. Same activations, same scale, opposite rankings depending solely on the optimizer. The whole activation canon — GELU, SwiGLU, all of it — was established with Adam. You can't assume those rankings carry over to Muon or other orthogonalizing optimizers, which is worth knowing given how much the optimizer landscape has shifted recently.
