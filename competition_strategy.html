Do better MLP activations help in Modded-NanoGPT — and does the answer depend on the optimizer?

Working notes. Private fork of KellerJordan/modded-nanogpt. Author: Adam Holmes. Status: Muon LR-robustness grid complete (2 seeds); NorMuon pre-screen complete. Gate-3 H100 run: NO-GO — all three candidates are equivalent under the production optimizer.

1. Background

Modded-NanoGPT is a community speedrun: train a ~124M-parameter GPT on the FineWeb text dataset to a fixed validation loss (cross-entropy ≤ 3.28 nats/token) as fast as possible on a fixed 8×H100 node. The data and target are frozen; everything else (architecture, optimizer, schedule) is fair game. The current record is ~1.4 minutes. The modern record stack uses the Muon optimizer, FP8 matmuls, sliding-window attention, and a ReLU² activation (max(0,x)²) in its MLP blocks.

Our question grew out of trying to beat that record by changing the activation: does the MLP activation / gating choice matter, and — the part that turned out to be the real story — does the best choice depend on which optimizer you train with? Goal: learn modern LLM-training engineering and produce a portfolio writeup, with or without a record.

2. Experimental framework

All numbers below come from a controlled local harness (one RTX 5060 Ti, 16 GB), built to rank design choices cheaply — not to reproduce the benchmark. Clearly:

Model: decoder-only GPT. Two scales used: medium (51M: 8 layers, width 512) for fast pilots, and small (124M: 12 layers, width 768, 6 heads) matching the real nanogpt-small, for the faithful study.
Optimizers: AdamW (per-parameter adaptive), Muon (orthogonalizes each weight-matrix update via the real "Polar Express" iteration), and NorMuon (Muon + the record's neuron-wise variance normalization).
Training: bf16, sequence length 1024, batch 16, 3000–4000 steps, 4–5 seeds per variant. All variants share seeds, so comparisons are paired (the per-seed loss difference has tiny noise, ~0.004, even though run-to-run loss varies ~0.05).
Metric: mean validation cross-entropy on FineWeb (nats/token; lower is better), reported as the seed-mean of the final loss, plus steps-to-reach-a-target-loss (how the speedrun is actually scored). Significance via paired t-tests.
Faithful to the benchmark: model size, Muon + Polar Express, torch.compile, chunked cross-entropy. Not included: FP8, value embeddings, sliding-window attention, the real 896→2048 data schedule. So it is a clean ranking instrument; real-benchmark validation needs rented H100s.

3. What we compared

The MLP block is either standard (W₂·act(W₁x)) or gated (W₃·[act(W₁x) ⊙ (W₂x)] — two linear projections multiplied, one passed through an activation; the basis of SwiGLU/GeGLU in modern LLMs). Activations tested: ReLU², GELU, SiLU, x|x| (signed square), bilinear (no activation — the gate is just a second linear branch), and variants with a learnable slope/curvature. (A piecewise-quadratic GELU-approximator, "TriLU," was our original hypothesis; it was the worst variant tried and is not discussed further.)

Under AdamW, three clean findings:
  • Gating wins decisively — every gated MLP beats every standard MLP (gap ~0.09 nats, p<10⁻⁵), despite slightly fewer parameters. The multiplicative interaction, not the activation shape, does the work.
  • Cheap, sharp activations win — the transcendental-free square-law gates (x|x|, ReLU²) beat the smooth GELU/SiLU gates, and even beat bilinear (no activation). Adding a slope term at the origin (x + x|x|) helps a further ~0.03. This is the best news for a speed benchmark: the most accurate gate is also the cheapest to fuse.
  • A bounded 0–1 "valve" gate loses badly (~0.09 worse): bounding the gate throws away the sign and gain a linear gate exploits, matching Shazeer 2020's GLU-variants result.

The leaders are same-cost standard MLPs. The top two are both 2-matmul standard MLPs that fix ReLU²'s gradient-starved origin:
  • relu2_s1 — squared-ReLU with a linear floor, ReLU(x)²+ReLU(x). On final loss −0.057 vs the incumbent (p=0.19, not yet significant), but on the benchmark-relevant metric — steps to reach a target loss — consistently ~10% faster and far more stable (hits val 5.9 at 2800–2900 steps every seed, vs ReLU²'s 2800–3400).

Both are transcendental-free and fusable — genuine drop-in candidates with record upside (the powered grid below refines this ranking and adds sniqu).

Deriving sniqu — the Self-Normalizing Inverse-and-Quadratic Union.

Motivation. eluquad matches the best standard MLP, but it is not scale-controlled. For a standard-normal input x ∼ N(0,1), its output has mean ≈ 0.71 and variance ≈ 3.19 — so every MLP layer multiplies the residual-stream scale by √3.19 ≈ 1.8× and adds a constant offset, which the following RMSNorm then has to spend capacity undoing. This matters more under Muon than under Adam: Muon orthogonalizes each weight-matrix update, and a non-zero activation mean injects a rank-1 "DC" direction into the activation matrix that orthogonalization treats as genuine signal, degrading conditioning. The fix is the idea behind SELU: choose the activation's free constants so that a unit-Gaussian input maps to a zero-mean, unit-variance output (a self-normalizing fixed point on the first two moments).

Derivation. Let h(x) be the raw eluquad shape (x+x² for x≥0, x/(1−x) for x<0). Compute its first two moments under x ∼ N(0,1) by Gauss–Hermite quadrature: μ = E[h] = 0.706, σ² = Var[h] = 3.188. Then define an affine output transform with shift β and scale λ:

sniqu(x) = λ (h(x) − β),    β = μ = 0.706,   λ = 1/σ = 1/1.786 = 0.560.

By construction this gives exactly E[sniqu] = 0 and Var[sniqu] = 1 (verified numerically). Two free parameters (scale, shift) pinned by two moment constraints — structurally the same recipe SELU uses to fix its (λ, α), except SELU tunes the negative-branch depth and keeps g(0)=0, whereas we keep eluquad's exact shape and add a DC offset (so sniqu(0) = −λβ ≈ −0.40, deliberately cancelling the positive-mean bias).

What it keeps — and what it can't. It inherits eluquad's C² smoothness (matched value, slope, and curvature at the origin — no derivative jump like ReLU², no kink like SELU) and its cost (one reciprocal, no transcendental). But it is only moment-2 normalized, not Gaussian: the quadratic branch makes it irreducibly heavy-tailed (excess kurtosis ≈ 10 even after normalization), whereas near-linear SELU is close to Gaussian. That residual non-Gaussianity is exactly the curvature we suspect helps — and the selu-vs-sniqu contrast is designed to test whether it does.

6. Why an optimizer reorders activations

An optimizer is not just a speed knob — it changes which architectural properties matter.

  • AdamW divides each weight's update by its own gradient magnitude, so it is forgiving of scale and conditioning. Architectures then compete on raw expressivity — and gating, which adds multiplicative interactions, wins.
  • Muon replaces each weight-matrix update with its nearest orthogonal matrix (it flattens the update's singular values). This is powerful but sensitive to the conditioning of the gradient, which the activation controls. Consequences seen here: (1) Muon optimizes the plain standard MLP far better (its loss drops from 6.93 to 6.20), closing the gap gating was filling; (2) it cannot freely rescale activations, so an activation's scale/sparsity now matters directly.

The practical upshot: activation/gating conclusions are optimizer-dependent. The entire activation canon — GELU (2016), SwiGLU (2020) — was established with Adam-family optimizers. Muon and related orthogonalizing optimizers (Shampoo, SOAP) postdate it, and our results show their rankings differ.

7. Final conclusions

  • Gating: ruled out (statistically significant). Under Muon at 124M every gated MLP is significantly worse than the incumbent ReLU² and costs more (p<0.05, n=4 paired seeds, all four variants). No rented-H100 budget goes here.
  • Standard-MLP variants: not a record opportunity. sniqu, relu2_s1, and eluquad all showed promising signals under plain Muon (sniqu ~−0.052, 2 seeds, consistent). But the NorMuon pre-screen closed the door: at the production optimizer all three land within 0.012 nats of each other — effectively tied. The activation swap does not survive the optimizer that actually matters. Neither candidate goes to rented H100s.
  • Infrastructure built and validated: Both candidates are integrated into the real train_gpt.py behind a one-line env flag, the fused Triton kernel supports all three activations with verified forward and backward passes, and a turnkey rented-H100 A/B harness is in deploy/. The work is not wasted — it would be trivial to A/B on the real benchmark if a future signal warrants it.
  • The statistically solid finding — and why it matters: Activation-function and gating rankings are optimizer-dependent. Gating beats standard MLPs by ~0.09 nats under AdamW and loses by 0.10–0.16 under Muon (p<0.05). NorMuon's variance normalization further washes out the finer ranking differences that appeared under plain Muon, suggesting the mechanism: orthogonalizing optimizers are intrinsically more robust to activation scale and conditioning than Adam-family optimizers, so activation choice matters less. The entire activation canon (GELU, SwiGLU) was benchmarked under Adam; these results show those rankings do not reliably transfer to the modern optimizer family.
  • To make it a paper would additionally need: multiple model scales, per-optimizer hyperparameter co-tuning, disentangling activation shape from MLP structure, and testing generality across the orthogonalizing-optimizer family (Muon, Shampoo, SOAP).
