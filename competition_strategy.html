1. Goals & Motivation

The dual goal of competing on modded-nanogpt is (a) to learn modern LLM training engineering deeply, and (b) to produce a portfolio artifact strong enough to back a job search in AI/ML roles.

Both goals point at the same activity but bias the choice of what to do:

  • For learning, depth beats breadth. Picking one or two areas (kernels, optimizers, activations) and going deep teaches more than mechanically porting twenty features.
  • For career signal, the visible artifact is a writeup — not a leaderboard line. A leaderboard entry is googleable proof of competence, but the blog post / X thread / PR description is what does the work in hiring conversations. Frontier-lab and ML-infra hiring managers recognize modded-nanogpt; outside that bubble (generic SWE), it’s a curiosity that requires context.

The honest signal hierarchy (approximate):

  1. Novel idea that becomes a leaderboard record (hard; weeks-to-months; may fail)
  2. Systematic empirical study published as a blog post, with new insight, even without a record (achievable, high payoff)
  3. Leaderboard record from careful ports + retuning + writeup (achievable, reasonable signal)
  4. Small but real systems-level contribution on Track 1 (signals strong ML engineering)
  5. Running existing code without a record or writeup (low signal)

Conclusion: aim for tier 2–3. Don’t aim for “any record at any cost.” Aim for an experiment whose results are interesting whether or not they yield a record.

2. Rules of the Competition

Modded-NanoGPT Rules — Index Card

    (Batch size, seq len, attention pattern: OK.)
  2. Hit target loss on FineWeb val (10.485M tokens):
      Track 1 (Small): ≤ 3.28
      Track 2 (Medium): ≤ 2.92
  3. Statistical significance: p < 0.01 that mean val loss meets target. Systems-only PRs exempt.
  4. No extra torch._inductor.config / torch.compile flags. Exception: coordinate_descent_tuning allowed on Medium.
  5. Must beat prior record on same hardware (8×H100, PrimeIntellect).
  6. Discretionary: don’t tank readability for tiny gains; don’t consume the ~0.001–0.002 loss buffer below target.
Otherwise: anything goes.

Expanded notes

The objective is mathematical. From the README: “the goal is to obtain a probability model of language which assigns a probability of at least math.exp(-3.28 * 10485760) to the first 10,485,760 tokens of the FineWeb valset.” Any architecture producing a valid joint probability over those tokens is admissible.

What this allows:

  • Arbitrary changes to num_layers, model_dim, num_heads, head_dim
  • Different activation functions, normalization schemes, gating
  • Different attention mechanisms (already used: FA3, long-short sliding window, paired-head)
  • Non-transformer architectures in principle (Mamba/SSM, RWKV, MoE, diffusion text models — though wallclock-competitive realization is very hard)
  • Multiple training passes with varying seq lengths (already done: schedule of 896 → 2048)
  • Multi-token prediction, auxiliary losses, curriculum, batch-size schedule (all already used)
  • Whatever optimizer you want (Muon currently dominates; alternatives are fair game)

What this excludes:

  • Changing the tokenizer (the TokenMonster rejection) — vocab is fixed at GPT-2 BPE, 50,257 tokens (padded to 50,304)
  • Re-tokenizing val or training on extra/different tokens
  • Validating on a subset of the val set
  • Test-time-training / parameter updates during eval with untimed backward passes (the @samacqua rejection)
  • Output distributions that don’t cover all 50,257 vocab IDs

No minimum speedup. A 5ms record is technically valid. But the discretionary rules cap how much code complexity is acceptable per ms (200 lines for 300ms: yes; 500 lines for 50ms: probably not), and protect the maintainer’s 0.001–0.002 loss buffer below the target so that validation runs remain cheap.

3. Hardware & Where the Time Goes

Official runs: 8 × H100 SXM5 on PrimeIntellect. Per-GPU: ~989 BF16 TFLOPs, ~1979 FP8 TFLOPs, 80 GB HBM3 at 3.35 TB/s, NVLink at ~900 GB/s. Aggregate: ~8 PFLOPs BF16 / ~16 PFLOPs FP8.

Current record: ~1.41 min on Track 1 (~84 s, ~1480 steps). Total training tokens: ~393 M (less than 1 epoch of the 10B FineWeb subset). The whole speedrun has been a data-efficiency competition: same val set, fewer tokens used.

Bottlenecks (from PR-level evidence and Akash’s “Profiling 101” writeup, linked at record #44):

  • Model body (attention + MLP): compute-bound. Hence FA3, sliding-window attention, fused Linear→ReLU²→Linear Triton kernel (record #59), FP8 matmul, mixed-precision Muon.
  • Embedding & LM head: bandwidth-bound. 50,304×768 matrices touched twice per step. Hence FP8 head, asymmetric rescale, softcap fusion, sparse bigram gradients.
  • Gradient all-reduce: NVLink-bound. Multiple records (22, 23, 24) improved this: reduce_scatter, async overlap, sparse for bigram.
  • Optimizer step: distributed Muon, Polar Express (replaces Newton-Schulz), batched/fused Adam, interweaved Adam/Muon.

The model body fits comfortably in HBM (~250 MB in BF16 for 125M params). The benchmark is not memory-capacity-bound, so techniques like layer-weight sharing (ALBERT-style) don’t help — they save params but not FLOPs.

B44-bit LM head with trainable per-output-token FP16 scales. The LM head (768 × 50304) is currently FP8 (records #19, #67). Going to 4-bit weights paired with trainable per-row FP16 scales (one scalar per vocab token) potentially halves HBM bandwidth on the bandwidth-bound LM-head weight read while preserving precision via the per-token scales (standard pattern in GPTQ / AWQ / SmoothQuant, but applied at training time rather than post-hoc).

Mechanism: 4-bit weight matrix (16 discrete values per element) × 16-bit per-row trainable scale = high effective precision per output token, since each row can be calibrated to its characteristic logit range. Differentiable end-to-end via straight-through estimator on the quantization step; the scales themselves train cleanly.

Estimated win: ~3–5% step wallclock if quality parity holds. Engineering effort: ~1–2 weeks for a custom INT4×BF16 fused kernel (reference implementations exist in Marlin, AWQ, vLLM’s INT4). Must integrate with existing softcap + asymmetric rescale + MTP CE fusion already present in the LM-head pipeline. Plausibly novel as a training-time application; tier-B leverage if TriLU’s Phase 1 establishes the activation experiment as worthwhile to pursue further.
B54-bit weights with per-row FP16 scales for gated MLP W₂ (gate matrix). Conditional on Phase 1 establishing gated MLPs (SwiGLU/GeGLU/TriGLU/xglu) as competitive. Same quantization pattern as B4, applied to the gate matrix in a gated MLP block. Each layer’s W₂ is 768 × 2048 (under the (8/3)d hidden-dim convention); going to 4-bit with per-row FP16 scales reduces W₂ memory by ~87% and bandwidth on the weight read by ~75% vs BF16 / ~50% vs FP8.

Why this works for gates specifically: gates in trained networks tend toward near-binary per-channel "on/off" decisions, so 16 discrete values per element + per-output-channel scales preserves the relevant decision boundaries while drastically reducing weight memory. AWQ / SmoothQuant routinely quantize gate matrices to 4-bit at inference without quality loss.

Estimated win: gate matmul ~30–50% faster → ~10–15% of MLP time → ~3–5% step wallclock. Engineering effort similar to B4 (~1–2 weeks). Could extend to W₁ (activation matrix) and W₃ (output matrix) for additive savings, though those have less binary-like behavior and may show more quality sensitivity.

Dependency chain: Phase 1 must first show gated MLPs viable. If Phase 1 ranks ReLU² / TriLU above SwiGLU / GeGLU / TriGLU, this idea is moot for modded-nanogpt.

C7Compressed gate input (pooled or truncated, for W₂ in gated MLPs). Reduce the gate input dimension before the W₂ matmul. Two variants in increasing risk-and-savings order:

(a) Naive truncation: gate sees only first d/k channels of input. W₂ becomes (d/k) × 2048. Saves k× on params and FLOPs, but hostage to whichever channels happen to carry important signal.

(b) Cheap pooling (recommended): group input channels into d/k groups, apply mean/max/L2 pooling within each group, then feed the pooled d/k-dim vector to W₂. Same gate matmul cost as (a), but the gate sees a compressed summary of all input channels rather than just a fraction. The pooling itself is essentially free (~0.3% overhead vs the gate matmul). This is structurally the Squeeze-and-Excitation pattern (Hu et al. 2018), which is proven to work in vision — SE blocks use global average pooling before a small MLP to compute per-channel attention scores.

Estimated savings at k=10:
  • Gate matmul FLOPs reduced ~90%
  • Gate matmul becomes ~3% of its original compute → MLP time drops ~27% → ~7–10% step wallclock if quality holds
  • Stacks with B5 (4-bit weights) for combined wallclock impact in the same range plus a memory win

Quality risk grows with k. At k=2 (half-input), risk is mild — gates still see substantial input info. At k=10, the gate is making decisions based on a 10×-compressed summary; quality can collapse if gate decisions need fine-grained per-channel info. Sweep k ∈ {2, 4, 8, 16} with pooled variant to find the threshold.

Pooling choice: max preserves dominant features per group; mean preserves overall magnitude; mean+max concat doubles gate input dim but preserves both. Fixed pooling (not learned) is the right choice if compute reduction is the goal — a learned d → d/k projection just moves the parameters elsewhere via low-rank decomposition.

Dependency: same as B5 — conditional on Phase 1 establishing gated MLPs as viable.

7. Detailed Plan: TriLU Activation Function Experiment

The TriLU activation family

TriLU (Three-piece Linear Unit) is a piecewise polynomial activation function with three regions: zero, quadratic, identity. Continuous (C°), with the transitions to the flat and linear pieces happening at learnable breakpoints. The general form has three parameters:

             ┌  0                                  if x ≤ L
TriLU(x;L,R,α) = ┤  α(x−L)(x−R) + R(x−L)/(R−L)        if L < x < R
             └  x                                  if x ≥ R

Equivalent to αx² + βx + γ in the middle, with β = R/(R−L) − α(L+R) and γ = αLR − LR/(R−L).

Two named special cases used in this plan:

  • Symmetric TriLU: L = −a, R = +a, curvature renamed κ. Two parameters per layer.
  • TriLU (asymmetric, full 3-parameter form): independent L, R, α.

Useful limits:

  • α = 0: piecewise linear — the “ramp-on” activation
  • α = 1/(4a) in the symmetric case: recovers the C¹ Huber smooth-ReLU
  • α > 1/(4a): GELU-like underbite (middle quadratic dips slightly negative before re-entering zero)
  • Minimax-best GELU fit: L ≈ −1.91, R ≈ +1.91, α ≈ 0.247

Motivation

ReLU² (max(0, x)²) has held the activation slot in modded-nanogpt since October 2024 (record #5). Properties:

  • Found via architecture search in Primer (So et al. 2021), matches GELU at lower FLOPs cost
  • No transcendentals; trivially fusable into a Triton kernel (record #59 fuses Linear→ReLU²→Linear)
  • Non-negative outputs; sparse-ish activations
  • Unbounded — grows quadratically for large positive x

The unboundedness is a real concern: it’s the kind of thing that forces careful init scaling, logit softcap, and FP8 dynamic-range management in the rest of the codebase. A bounded-slope alternative could in principle simplify the surrounding scaffolding (and pair well with C4 above).

Cost and parameterization

TriLU cost vs ReLU²: ~4–5 ops/element vs 2 (two extra compares for the asymmetric form, one extra multiply, one extra add). Parameter count: 2 per layer (symmetric) or 3 per layer (asymmetric), optionally per-channel. All negligible.

Soft-positivity for the breakpoint magnitudes:

# symmetric TriLU
a     = F.softplus(theta_a) + eps    # transition half-width > 0
kappa = F.softplus(theta_k)          # curvature ≥ 0

# asymmetric TriLU
L     = -F.softplus(theta_L) - eps   # left breakpoint < 0
R     =  F.softplus(theta_R) + eps   # right breakpoint > 0
alpha = F.softplus(theta_a)          # curvature ≥ 0

Initialize at the minimax-fit GELU values (see initialization options below) and let the optimizer drift. Per-layer learnable scalars by default; consider per-channel as a follow-up if per-layer shows meaningful inter-layer variation.

Use a separate, smaller learning rate for the activation parameters — typically 0.1× the main LR. This is standard practice for scalar learned parameters (layer-norm scales, learned positional embeddings, log-variance in VAEs, etc.) and prevents the activation shape from being jerked around by the optimizer chasing weight updates.

Layer weight initialization

Distinct from activation parameter initialization: this is about how the MLP weights W₁, W₂ are drawn.

  • 0.5 × model_dim-0.5 (current codebase value)
  • √2 × model_dim-0.5 (vanilla Kaiming for GELU)
  • 0.7 × model_dim-0.5 (split the difference)

This is part of the Tier C4 “co-tuning” angle: if TriLU’s GELU-like shape rewards a different init than ReLU²’s unbounded shape did, that’s where the extra leverage lives.

The recommended setup runs TriLU and TriGLU with parameters frozen at the minimax GELU-fit values (see “Frozen vs learnable TriLU” below). This answers, in a single sweep:

  1. Does activation shape matter? (ReLU² vs GELU vs TriLU in the standard-MLP row)
  2. Does gating matter? (GELU vs GeGLU; TriLU vs TriGLU)
  3. Do they compose? (TriGLU vs SwiGLU/GeGLU)

6 variants × 3 seeds × ~40 min = ~12 hr total on RTX 5060 Ti.

Why TriGLU is in the default sweep

SwiGLU (SiLU in a gated MLP) is the standard activation/MLP combo in modern open LLMs (Llama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek). It is conspicuously absent from modded-nanogpt — ReLU² in a non-gated MLP has held since record #5 (Oct 2024). The gated form’s formula:

GatedMLP(x) = W₂ · ( act(W₁·x) ⊙ (V·x) )

The element-wise multiplication (gating) lets one channel modulate another — multiplicative interactions that pure pointwise activations can’t express. To keep param count matched, gated variants use hidden dim (8/3)d instead of 4d: three matrices of size d × (8/3)d = same 8d² as two matrices of size d × 4d.

TriGLU — TriLU inside the gated MLP structure — composes the two hypotheses (bounded GELU-shape activation, multiplicative gating). It is genuinely interesting only if TriLU’s learnable parameters contribute meaningfully beyond “approximating GELU”: if Phase 1’s trajectory plots show (L, R, α) drifting to layer-dependent values, that’s evidence the learnable shape matters and TriGLU is the natural Phase 3 follow-up. If trained parameters stay near the GELU-fit init, TriGLU ≈ GeGLU ≈ SwiGLU.

Frozen vs learnable TriLU: the cleanest hypothesis test

The cleanest variant is frozen TriLU at the minimax GELU-fit values — (L, R, α) initialized at (−1.910, +1.910, 0.247) and never updated. Zero learnable activation parameters.

Why: every existing activation in modded-nanogpt is fixed (ReLU², GELU, SiLU all have no params). A like-for-like test of “GELU-shape vs ReLU²-shape at near-equal cost” should also have no learnable params, otherwise any win is confounded between “the shape itself” and “the network found a better shape via the learnable knobs.”

4-piece extension (considered, deferred)

A natural generalization: a 4-piece function with an explicit linear underbite segment between the zero and quadratic regions:

             ┌  0                       if x ≤ a
4piece(x) =  │  m(x − a)                 if a < x < b   (linear, slope m, anchored at (a, 0))
             │  αx² + βx + γ          if b < x < c   (quadratic, C° to neighbors)
             └  x                        if x ≥ c

5 learnable parameters: a, b, c, m, α. With m < 0 this models GELU's negative-side underbite explicitly.

Why we’re deferring this:

  • Same hypothesis, marginally better fit. The 4-piece doesn’t test a meaningfully different hypothesis than TriLU — it’s the same “GELU-shape vs ReLU²” question with a 25% closer approximation. If TriLU at GELU-fit init beats ReLU², the differential improvement from 4-piece will likely be lost in noise. If TriLU loses, 4-piece probably loses too.
  • Diminishing returns. ReLU² (0 params) → TriLU (3 params) → 4-piece (5 params) → ... at some point you’re building a spline approximation to GELU and should just use GELU. The 4-piece is the last useful step before that.
  • Kernel complexity scales. 4 regions vs 3 means one extra compare per element and 5 scalar inputs to the fused kernel instead of 3. Not catastrophic, but adds friction for a marginal gain.
  • The underbite TriLU already provides may be enough. TriLU at κ = 0.247 > 1/(4×1.91) = 0.131 is in the underbite regime — the quadratic dips below zero (min ≈ −0.20 at x ≈ −1.01). It's not a great underbite fit (GELU's true min is −0.170 at x ≈ −0.752), but it's nonzero, and the 4-piece's explicit linear segment doesn't dramatically improve underbite-region fidelity either.

When to revisit: if Phase 1 TriLU trajectory plots show the network wanting a deeper or differently-positioned underbite than TriLU can produce, the 4-piece becomes a natural follow-up. Until that signal exists, the added complexity isn’t justified.

Strategic motivation: TriLU at GELU-fit init

The right framing isn’t “TriLU vs. symmetric TriLU.” The right framing is TriLU (or symmetric TriLU) initialized to approximate GELU vs. ReLU².

The strategic case:
  • ReLU² was selected in this codebase (record #5) as a slight quality win over GELU, not a dramatic shape change. The inline comment in record #5’s code reads: “~1-2% better than GELU” (citing the Primer paper, arxiv 2109.08668).
  • Modern LLMs (Llama, Mistral, GPT-4 family) overwhelmingly use GELU or SwiGLU. ReLU² is a niche choice for this wallclock benchmark.
  • TriLU at the minimax-fit init (L = −1.91, R = +1.91, α = 0.247) tracks GELU within max error 0.053 / RMS 0.029.
  • Cost: ~5 ops/element vs ReLU²’s ~2. Inside the fused MLP kernel, activation cost is a small fraction (~2–5%) of matmul cost. The wallclock overhead is ~1–3% per MLP block.
This is the cleanest possible test of the hypothesis "GELU shape vs ReLU² shape at near-equal cost." The expected magnitude is modest (see below).
Calibrated expectation (sobering reality check). If record #5’s inline comment is right that ReLU² is "~1-2% better than GELU," then TriLU at GELU-fit (which approximates GELU within ~0.05) recovers at most ~1-2% relative to ReLU². Net expected wallclock improvement after accounting for the fused-kernel cost: 0–2%.

For context: recent Track 1 records improve by 50–200 ms out of ~85 seconds, i.e. 0.1–0.25% per record. So a 1–2% net win would be a large record by current standards. But the margin is tight enough that:

  • Co-tuning matters disproportionately (Phase 1.5) — a 1–2% effect is the same magnitude as casual hyperparameter mis-tuning
  • The loss-buffer rule constrains how the win must be claimed (fewer steps at same loss, not lower loss at same steps)

Symmetric vs. asymmetric: an empirical finding

The L² (and minimax) best fit of TriLU to GELU is exactly symmetric (L = −R). The reason: GELU satisfies GELU(0) = 0 with slope 1/2 at the origin; when L = −R in the TriLU family, the linear coefficient β is forced to exactly 1/2, so any best-fit through (0, 0) with slope 1/2 lands symmetric.

So for the GELU-init starting point, asymmetric TriLU and symmetric TriLU are equivalent. The asymmetric form’s extra degree of freedom matters only if the network drifts to asymmetric breakpoints during training. Run both as separate experiments — if TriLU’s trained (L, R) stay symmetric, symmetric TriLU was sufficient; if not, the third parameter pays for itself.

Why GELU specifically, not SiLU/Swish

SwiGLU (SiLU in a gated MLP) is the modern LLM standard, so a reasonable question is whether TriLU should target SiLU instead of GELU. The numerical answer settles this: TriLU is structurally a GELU approximator, not a SiLU approximator.

Implication for the experiment design:

  • TriLU competes with GELU. Frozen TriLU at GELU-fit is genuinely a GELU stand-in. The Phase 1 standard-MLP row tests ReLU² vs GELU vs TriLU as three nearly-equivalent-shape candidates.
  • TriGLU competes with GeGLU. Same activation shape (GELU-like), same gating structure. This is the cleanest gated-MLP comparison.
  • Neither competes with SiLU/SwiGLU directly. SwiGLU is included as a modern LLM reference point in the gated row but tests a different shape entirely. A win for SwiGLU over GeGLU/TriGLU would suggest SiLU's shape matters; a win for TriGLU/GeGLU over SwiGLU would suggest the SiLU vs GELU difference doesn't matter (and the lower-cost activation wins).
  • If SiLU's shape turns out to matter (SwiGLU clearly above GeGLU and TriGLU in Phase 1), the 4-piece extension is a better path to a SiLU stand-in than TriLU — its explicit linear underbite segment can accommodate SiLU's wider negative tail. Reserved as a Phase 1.5 follow-up.

The historical context also favors GELU: it's the activation that ReLU² actually replaced in this codebase (record #5, Oct 2024). The cleanest hypothesis test is the direct historical question.

Initialization options

  • GELU-fit init (minimax, recommended): symmetric TriLU at a = 1.910, κ = 0.247; TriLU at L = −1.910, R = +1.910, α = 0.247. Minimizes worst-case deviation from GELU: max abs error 0.053, RMS error 0.029.
  • GELU-fit init (L²/RMS): symmetric TriLU at a = 1.749, κ = 0.275; TriLU at L = −1.749, R = +1.749, α = 0.275. Lower RMS (0.025) but higher worst-case (0.070).
  • C¹ init: symmetric TriLU at a = 1, κ = 0.25; TriLU at L = −1, R = 1, α = 0.25. Starts at the smooth (no-kink) point.
  • ReLU init: symmetric TriLU at small a (e.g. 0.1), TriLU at small R−L. Starts close to ReLU with a narrow smoothing region.

Use the minimax-fit init by default. For function approximation, L² (RMS) is the wrong objective — its statistical justification (MLE under Gaussian noise) doesn’t apply when fitting a deterministic target. Minimax (Chebyshev) is the classical choice and bounds the worst-case deviation at any single x, which is the operationally relevant quantity for an activation function. The minimax fit reduces worst-case error by 24% relative to the L² fit, with the residual showing perfect equi-oscillation at 5 alternating extrema of magnitude ≈0.053.

Phase 1.5: Co-tuning hyperparameters around TriLU (single H100, ~$50–100)

Methodological caveat addressed here. Phase 1 holds hyperparameters constant across variants — useful as a quick filter, but a clean "is TriLU better than ReLU²" test requires both activations to be at their own tuned hyperparameters. The current modded-nanogpt setup (init std 0.5/√d, logit softcap, specific Muon LR, certain gate inits) was co-tuned around ReLU²’s shape and unboundedness. Dropping TriLU in with all of these unchanged measures "TriLU at ReLU²-tuned settings," not "TriLU at its best."

This is a known problem in ML benchmark methodology — hyperparameter coupling. The classical demonstration is “Bag of Tricks for Image Classification” (He et al. 2019) which showed that the apparent ranking of architectures often changes dramatically when each gets its own tuned hyperparameters.

What to hold constant: zero-init projections (this is structural, not activation-specific), gate inits, batch size schedule, sliding window schedule, attention parameters. These aren’t strongly coupled to activation choice.

Sweep design (smart, not exhaustive):

  • Don’t do a full grid — that’s 4 × 3 × 3 = 36 configs × 3 seeds = expensive
  • Instead: vary one hyperparameter at a time around the ReLU²-tuned values, picking the best. ~12 configs × 3 seeds = 36 runs.
  • Run on single H100 at reduced step count (~500 steps instead of full Track 1 1480) since we’re looking for relative ranking, not absolute target hit.
  • Cost estimate: $1–2/run on Lambda single-H100 = $50–100 total.

Output: a "TriLU-tuned hyperparameter set" to use in Phase 3 validation. The fair comparison in the eventual PR is then (ReLU² at its tuned setup) vs (TriLU at its tuned setup), not the naive drop-in.

Three possible Phase 1.5 outcomes:

  • TriLU wins at matched settings AND at tuned settings: unambiguous. Proceed to Phase 2 with confidence.
  • TriLU ties/loses at matched but wins at tuned: the more interesting outcome — demonstrates the surrounding scaffolding was ReLU²-specific. Strong writeup material; proceed to Phase 2.
  • TriLU loses at both: definitive negative result. Don’t spend Phase 2/3 budget; pivot or write up the negative result.

Phase 2: Fused kernel (single H100, ~$20–100)

For TriLU variants that beat or tie ReLU² in Phase 1, write a fused Linear→TriLU→Linear Triton kernel matching the existing record-#59 kernel. Key implementation notes:

  • (L, R, α) (or (a, κ) for symmetric) need to flow in as small extra tensors (per-layer or per-channel)
  • Backward needs the activation derivative; piecewise: 0 if x ≤ L; 2αx + β if L < x < R; 1 if x ≥ R (where β = R/(R−L) − α(L+R))
  • Gradients w.r.t. L, R, α need to be accumulated across the batch×seq dimension
  • Stick with the existing kernel’s tile sizes and SRAM layout; benchmark against ReLU² on identical input shapes

Estimated effort: ~half a day to a day. Validate numerically against an unfused PyTorch reference.

Phase 3: Full validation (8×H100 PrimeIntellect)

Only if Phase 2 wallclock is within ~5% of ReLU². The two-track deployment strategy below covers how this phase splits.

Track 1 vs Track 2 deployment strategy

Earlier framing said “the experiment lives on Track 2.” That was overcautious. Deploy Track 1 first, then Track 2 in rapid succession. Rationale:

  • Track 1 iteration is 3–4× cheaper: $2–3/run vs $7–8/run. You can afford more exploratory runs to nail the right kernel tile sizes and init values.
  • Track 1 validation is also cheaper: a full p<0.01 sweep is ~$20–60 vs $60–160.
  • A Track 1 record is much stronger validation of the technique. Track 1 is the actively contested track. Clearing it against @classiclarryd / @YouJiacheng / AI systems is a much harder signal to dismiss.
  • The kernel is reusable. Same fused Linear→TriLU→Linear kernel transfers from Track 1 to Track 2; only the schedule and surrounding scaffolding need retuning.

  • A Track 2 record (strong portfolio item; Track 2 has been stagnant for 5+ months)
  • A clean writeup: “TriLU at GELU-fit init: tested on Track 1 (didn’t clear, here’s the analysis), worked on Track 2 (record + per-layer ablation)”

Strategically near-best-case: the activation that is cheapest to fuse (no transcendentals, existing kernel) is also the most accurate, so the gated-MLP kernel target is now concrete — a ReLU²-gate.
Finding 5 — xglu (gated x|x|) is the best gate, beating reglu (5 seeds, p<0.01). The second cheap gate, xglu = gated x|x| (sign(x)·x²; also transcendental-free and fusable), wins on all 5 seeds: mean 6.8354 vs reglu 6.8487, paired Δ = +0.0133, t=5.07, p=0.0072. This inverts the standard-MLP lesson: in a gated MLP, using the negative half of the input (x|x|, no sparsity) beats zeroing it (ReLU²) — plausibly because the gate already handles selection, so the activation branch benefits from passing more information rather than sparsifying. Two honest caveats: the effect is small (0.013 nats, vs reglu’s ~0.04 lead over the smooth gates), and p=0.0072 is the closest of the gated contrasts — it clears 0.01 but would not survive aggressive multiple-comparison correction. The kernel target stays a cheap polynomial gate either way (x|x| is just x*x.abs()).

Caveat (init fairness). All variants use the shared default nn.Linear init, which is not each activation’s variance-optimal init — so this is a fair drop-in comparison, not yet a pure shape comparison. LayerNorm launders much of the mismatch, and ReLU² is arguably the activation the generic init suits least (no closed-form init), so its win is hard to dismiss as init-luck. A small init-gain sweep (reglu vs xglu at 2–3 init scales) is the clean way to separate shape from init before claiming the mechanistic result; queued as a follow-up.

Activation-shape sweep (slope floor + curvature) — partial results

Both winning gates are quadratic, so both have a gradient-starved flat spot at the origin: ReLU² has derivative 2x → 0 as x→0⁺, and x|x| has derivative 2|x| → 0 from both sides. Small pre-activations learn slowly. The fix is a linear term giving a nonzero slope s at the origin; a curvature coefficient c scales the quadratic term. Both leave cost and fusability unchanged:

Finding 6 — the slope floor helps, monotonically, and reshuffles the top. reglu improves cleanly with slope: 6.8487 (s=0) → 6.8328 (s=0.5) → 6.8202 (s=1). Fixing ReLU²’s gradient-starved slow-start at the origin is a real, monotone win. Adding the same slope floor to x|x| gives xglu s=1 = QuadLU (x+x|x|) = 6.8076, the overall best — it edges reglu s=1 (6.8202) by 0.0126, but only at p=0.05 (not significant at 0.01): with both gates given the slope floor, the negative half (x|x| vs ReLU²) is a near-tie. Conversely, adding sparsity via the threshold shift (t=0.5) hurts (6.8531): slope good, extra sparsity bad. The kernel target is a slope-1 square-law gate — QuadLU (x+x|x|) or, near-equivalently, ReLU(x)+ReLU(x)².

QuadGLU: a square-law gated unit

The cheap-gate and slope-floor results converge on a specific unit worth naming and testing on its own. Define the value activation

QuadLU(x) = x + c·x|x| — linear near the origin (slope-1 floor: healthy small-x gradients), square-law growth in the tails, signed.

and its gated form QuadGLU = QuadLU(W₁x) · gate(W₂x). The name rides on the one robust, novel, structural feature — the square-law value branch (validated by Findings 5–6) — while the gate type and the curvature c are configurations described below, not part of the identity.

Gate type. The standard SwiGLU choice is a linear gate (signed, unbounded). An alternative is a clean division of labor: a bounded 0–1 gate decides whether a unit is on (carrying no magnitude), and QuadLU decides the output when on. For cost, the bounded gate can be a cheap rational valve ½·x/(1+|x|) + ½ (softsign-based; no transcendental, fusable, with gentle polynomial-tail saturation so gates do not die).

The transistor intuition (motivation, not the name). The analogy is unusually precise for the value branch: a MOSFET in saturation has drain current I_D ∝ (V_GS − V_th)² above threshold and 0 below — exactly a shifted squared-ReLU. So the square-law value is the FET characteristic, and the valve-gated form maps onto a field-effect transistor: the gate controls whether current flows; the channel (QuadLU) sets how much. It is an intuition pump, not a derivation — it strains where QuadLU is signed/bidirectional (transistor current is unipolar), and it motivates only the bounded-gate configuration, which the evidence may show is the weaker arm. Hence we keep “transistor” as motivation in prose and name the unit after its math.

The catch (and the prediction). A bounded 0–1 gate cannot invert the sign or amplify (>1); with it the output sign is sign(QuadLU(x)) alone, whereas a linear gate gives sign(QuadLU)·sign(gate) — two independent sign sources plus gain. Both Shazeer’s “GLU Variants Improve Transformer” (2020) and our own sweep favor the linear (unbounded) gate, so the clean division of labor is probably a constraint, not a feature. The queued gate-type A/B tests this directly — QuadLU value branch held fixed (c=1), gate varied across {linear, fast-sigmoid, true sigmoid} — isolating exactly whether bounding the gate helps or hurts. Expected outcome: linear-gate QuadGLU wins; the valve-gated QuadGLU is the instructive comparison arm and a clean writeup vignette either way.

Shape and initialization: two separate jobs

The curvature c and the gate initialization answer different questions and should be set independently:

  • Shape (c) — tune for the eventual data. c is a fixed function parameter, the same at step 0 and at convergence, so set it for where the network spends most of its life: the trained state, where the gate-input is ~N(0,1). For the bounded valve (E[gate²]≈0.30 at that distribution) the variance-preserving value is c ≈ 0.505 — the output of a standard-normal input has variance 1. (This is the full odd QuadLU x+0.505·x|x|; distinct from the half-zeroed reglu control’s c=0.253, a different value-branch form.)
  • Initialization — set for fast early convergence. Zero-init the gate matrix so every valve starts at g=0 → gate=0.5, the maximum-derivative point: strongest early gradient, agnostic (no presupposed open/closed), and symmetry broken by the random value matrix W₁. Pair with LeCun W₁,W₂ = 1/√fan_in (so the activation input really is ~N(0,1), the premise the c tuning assumes) and a variance-matched output W₃ that cancels QuadLU’s gain.

Because these are decoupled, the variance trajectory is benign: at init the zero-init gate is a constant 0.5 (E[gate²]=0.25), so output variance starts at ~0.84 (mildly under — the safe, ReZero-like direction: signals start small and grow); as the valve spreads toward N(0,1) over training, variance rises to 1.0 exactly — unit-variance where it matters, with a steep, agnostic, fast-learning start. The alternative (tuning c for the init state, c=0.6) is exact at step 0 but ~20% over for the whole rest of training, so the eventual-tuned c=0.505 is preferred.

Caveat on “variance 1”: it assumes the activation input is truly N(0,1), i.e. LeCun W₁ init. The local harness uses default PyTorch init (input ~N(0,1/3)), so locally we are really testing whether the lower-curvature shape helps; literal variance-preservation (which matters with Muon, where scale is not freely absorbed) is a Phase-1.5 concern with the proper init.

Held for the round after: the SE-style gate-pooling sweep (C7, k∈{4,8,16}); the init-gain fairness sweep; and Bilinear (Shazeer 2020) — the no-activation gated MLP (W₁x) · (W₂x). Bilinear is the cheapest gated variant (two matmuls + elementwise product, no activation), is naturally variance-preserving (product of two independent unit-variance projections has variance 1, no amplification — needs no W₃ downscale, good for Muon), yet still leptokurtic (kurtosis 9, the good tail regime). It is the clean ablation of our whole gating result: does the value-branch activation (ReLU²/x|x|) add anything beyond the multiplicative gating itself? Pooling is ~orthogonal to the activation shape (it acts on the linear gate branch), so the final config combines the best shape with the best pooling factor.

  • B5 / C7 dependency conditions are met. Both were gated on “Phase 1 establishing gated MLPs as viable.” They are now live: 4-bit gated-W₂ quantization (B5) and SE-style compressed gate input (C7) become candidate follow-ons if the gated kernel survives Phase 1.5.

One non-obvious consideration: the Track 1 buffer rule

Discretionary rule #2 (the loss buffer) interacts with all of these in the same way: any new technique must produce a wallclock saving at equivalent loss, not a loss improvement at equivalent wallclock. A 5% step time saving with 0.001 worse loss may still be net-negative for the record after reducing step count to recover the buffer.

This is why the realistic expected values above are below the naive "FLOPs / bandwidth saved" numbers — some of the saving gets re-invested in extra steps to maintain the buffer position.

10. Risks & Open Questions

Risks

  • Maintainer queues bulk transfer. If you spend $200 on a feature port and the maintainer ships their own bulk update, your PR sits. Mitigation: open a GH Discussion before doing significant validation work. Frame as “I’m working on this; is it stepping on your roadmap?”
  • Track 2 validation cost compounds. Each idea you validate at Medium is $60–160. Three failed ideas burns half your budget. Mitigation: single-H100 Phase-2 testing for every idea before paying for 8×H100 Phase-3 validation.
  • Not all Track 1 wins transfer. The bulk-transfer author noted “some features included are probably detrimental and don’t scale below 3.28 loss.” Mitigation: ablate. Run with and without each ported feature. Treat negative results as findings.
  • Muon orthogonalization makes activation scale non-absorbable. Adam can rescale weights to compensate for activation magnitude; Muon can’t as freely. This means activation choices interact with the optimizer in ways that don’t hold in Adam-only codebases. Mitigation: normalize activations explicitly; don’t rely on absorption.
  • Compile time eats iteration speed. torch.compile can take 5–7 minutes cold. Plan kernels and configs so compile artifacts amortize. Avoid rebuilding cached graphs unnecessarily.
  • Inter-run variance is real. p<0.01 isn’t free — some changes look like records on a single run and wash out across seeds. Always run ≥3 seeds before celebrating.
